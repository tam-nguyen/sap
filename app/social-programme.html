Celebrating the Golden Twenties - An Evening at The Secret Garden

15:00 | The Secret Garden

‘Enchanting’, ‘mysterious’ and ‘diverse’ are just some of the words used to describe our location for Saturday evening – and when you arrive, you will see why. The Secret Garden is a former cabaret theatre nestled in the heart of Berlin’s diverse Mitte neighbourhood and harks back to 1905 when Berlin was considered the ‘centre of Europe’ for entertainment.

Known in its heyday as the ‘Hummingbird Cabaret’, this beautiful landmark was one of the liveliest venues during Germany’s Golden Twenties era and sadly, it fell into a state of disrepair during the war. However, in 2008, this treasured venue was rediscovered, and a real estate company decided to give it a new lease of life. The restored structure you see today is a remarkable example of regeneration in the city and your evening here will be filled with music, dancing, great networking and superb dining. The menu for tonight comes from the three-Michelin starred chef Dieter Müller, known for his famed Amuse bouche menu and multiple international awards. We will close the night with an intimate performance by the man known as the ‘greatest living guitarist,’ the Grammy Award-winning, José Feliciano.

The Secret Garden

If ever there was a place to recapture the magic of Germany’s Golden Twenties era, it’s The Secret Garden in Berlin-Mitte. If the walls of this venue in the Gartenstrasse could speak, they’d share fascinating tales that hark back to some of Berlin’s most vibrant moments in history.

Today, The Secret Garden hosts the most prestigious events and musical performances. And this evening’s exclusive event will allow us to relive the charm that this former variety theatre brought to Berlin during its most convivial era. Originally built in 1904 by Oscar Garbe, an architect and entrepreneur, it was a hub of entertainment in the thriving Latin Quarter – then part of the Soviet Union-occupied East Berlin – and went under the name of Fritz Schmidt’s restaurant and Kolibri Festsäle. When Berlin entered its lively twenties era, it was known locally as The Hummingbird ballroom and remained a sought-after cabaret venue for another decade. When the war hit, furniture was taken to be used as firewood and it fell into a state of disrepair. In the words of the current owners, The Hummingbird ballroom ‘sank into oblivion’. No one could have imagined that this iconic landmark would ever be so glamorous again.

The man who had the vision was Dirk Moritz, founder and CEO of the Moritz Group, who stumbled upon the ruined building’s abandoned backyard while visiting a public swimming pool in 2008. The once-opulent venue was now a dilapidated shell filled with sofas and rubbish, but the entrepreneur saw through the 30 tons of rubble and debris and set about acquiring the site, so he could return the three-storey venue to its former glory.

By 2011 his dream began to become a reality when he secured the property and four years later, restoration began. With financial backing from a private bank in Hamburg, the Moritz Group spent $1.7m transforming the ballroom into a modern arts venue and gallery space.

Going by the philosophy ‘the waking of a ruin to new life,’ The Secret Garden was created with thoughtful restoration and sustainability in mind. As you marvel at the stucco fragments, elegant bannisters, murals, beams and sweeping archways, you’ll see a beautiful illustration of how sensitive restoration can bring back the magic of a historical site.

Today, sleek events rooms span across four floors and on Saturday night, The Secret Garden will take you back to the Golden Twenties era when Berlin became the third largest municipality in the world. Accessed via a discreet entrance on Potsdamer Straße, we hope you’ll agree, it possesses a charming and ‘secret’ quality and serves as the perfect place for our evening celebrations.

Dieter Mueller

Dieter Müller is one of Germany’s most revered chefs and is considered by many as the country’s best. He became the first gourmet chef to open a restaurant on board a cruise ship and has picked up multiple international awards during his outstanding career.

Born in Baden, Müller grew up in the Black Forest and began cooking at the age of eight in his parents’ catering business, following an apprenticeship at the Hotel Bauer in Baden-Württemberg in southern Germany.

In the early 1970s, he took the next step in his culinary career when he took on a position at the hotel Schweizerhof. Under the direction of former champion chef Ernesto Schlegel, Müller gleaned skills in classic French cuisine and in 1972, he jetted off to the island of Corfu to take on a new role at the sister property, the Hotel Miramare Beach.

A year later, he returned to home soil to take the helm of the restaurant Schweizer Stuben, in Wertheim, in the north of Baden-Wurttemberg. He ran the restaurant with his brother Jörg for 17 years and passed his final chef exam while he was there. As Maître Chef de Cuisine, he attracted food aficionados to this small-town restaurant from all corners of the globe and earned two Michelin stars for their ‘nouvelle cuisine’. He also picked up the ‘Chef of the Year’ award twice, published his first (award-winning) cookbook and was named one of the top 16 chefs in the world. He was also awarded 19.5 points by Gault Millau.

In 1992, Chef Müller opened the elegant château restaurant, Schlosshotel Lerbach, near Cologne. And just five years later, the Michelin Guide awarded him three stars. Chef Müller is the first German chef to receive four Hauben (Chef’s Hats) from Gault Millau and in 1998, the gourmet magazine, ‘Der Feinschmecker’ named him Chef of the Year.

In 2010, Müller’s career took another interesting turn when he opened “Restaurant Dieter Müller,” his own gourmet restaurant on board the MS EUROPA cruise ship, where he works 70 days a year.

Known for his superb cooking school, modest charm and philosophy to ‘cook from the heart’, Chef Müller has become a strong part of Germany’s culinary culture.

The Reichstag

One of the most impressive sights here in Berlin is, of course, The Reichstag. Located on the southern banks of the River Spree between the Brandenburg Gate and Central Station, this prestigious landmark, home to the German government, rises like a beacon above the Berlin skyline and has witnessed some of the most momentous events in European history. The historic edifice and its striking glass dome have become an iconic image the world over and today it remains Berlin’s second most popular attraction, pulling in some 3 million visitors each year.

Read more

Construction of The Reichstag, inspired by the Memorial Hall in Philadelphia, began in the late 19th Century and it was finally completed some 23 years later, thanks to German architect Paul Wallot. The final structure divided opinions at the time for its eclectic blend of architectural designs, but with its glass cupola, imposing columns and decorative motifs, it was one of the country’s most remarkable feats of engineering and became the official home of the Imperial Diet of the German Empire.

The Reichstag fire, an arson attack which took place some 40 years later, devastated the building, and at the time, many believed that Nazi military leader Hermann Wilhelm Goring orchestrated the fire in order to consolidate Hitler’s rise to power. Historians today, however, believe that 24-year-old Dutch bricklayer Marinus van der Lubbe, was in fact the ‘communist culprit’ behind the blaze. By the time the Second World War hit, The Reichstag had become an empty, derelict shell with bombs leaving the once-opulent building in ruins.

When the Berlin Wall was constructed, and Germany was divided by the Cold War, The Reichstag lay within West Berlin, just a few metres away from the divide. Various repairs took place on the ruined building, but it wasn’t until the 1990s – when the country reunified and governmental powers were transferred from Bonn to Berlin – that the German parliament, known as the Bundestag was formed and a full restoration began.

It was British architect Norman Foster who was finally appointed to carry out this historical make-over and the innovator behind architectural wonders such as the UK’s Wembley Stadium gained worldwide interest for doing so. The modern Reichstag building would boast the myriad Renaissance, Postmodern and Baroque Revival architecture it was known for, however, this new-and-improved monument would draw attention for its technologically innovative designs and become an inspiration to artists and architects from all over the world.

Those who come to The Reichstag today are blown away by the iconic dome which creates a visual spectacle inside and out. Spiralling interior walkways offer 360-degree views across the city and the

chamber is filled with ever-changing streams of light, thanks to a central mirrored cone. Equally impressive are its energy efficient credentials (it is powered by both solar and wind energy and amazingly, vegetable oil) and hi-tech designs ensure a steady climate within the copula year-round. Whether you enjoy a glass of sparkling wine from the impressive roof terrace or enjoy the panorama at night, you will understand why this visual spectacle symbolises reunification and democracy, as well as a positive future for Berlin.

An Evening at Axica with Special Guest German Chancellor Angela Merkel

15:00 | Conference Room 101

This evening will be hosted at the spectacular Axica, a prestigious Berlin landmark designed by the famous American Architect, Frank O. Gehry. What makes this venue so special, aside from its breathtaking atrium is its location in the Pariser Platz, right next to Berlin’s historic Brandenburg Gate. After marvelling at this Neoclassical monument, we will step inside Axica where you will have the opportunity to marvel at the building’s expansive glass façade which has inspired architects and artists the world over for its extraordinary design.

Read more

Thanks to pioneers such as Gehry, Berlin’s cityscape now boasts an eclectic blend of old and new and we are honoured to welcome German Chancellor Angela Merkel this evening who will give a formal address on the topic of ‘Europe’s Role in the World.’ The Chancellor will take your questions and our glamourous evening will be topped off with Axica’s superb hospitality, featuring an exquisite menu by Tim Raue. The Berlin-born chef who began his career at the Quadriga restaurant in the Brandenburger Hotel, is renowned in the culinary world for spanning across both Asian fusion and German Prussian cuisine. The menu at his eponymous Michelin-starred restaurant here in Berlin takes influences from Japanese, Thai and Chinese cultures and local food writers have named him the ‘best chef in Berlin.’

Optional Programme for Partners – An Exclusive Tour of Potsdam and Museum Barberini

Hosted by Julie McDermott | 15:00 | Conference Room 101

Axica, our venue for Sunday evening is among the most unusual modern structures in Berlin. Located within the atrium of the DZ Bank building at the Brandenburg Gate, it was designed by Frank O. Gehry, the illustrious Canadian architect behind some of the world’s most famous modern building designs.

Read more

With a diverse portfolio that includes the likes of the Fondation Louis Vuitton in Paris, the Guggenheim Museum in Bilbao and the Walt Disney Concert Hall in LA, Gehry’s creation for Berlin’s Pariser Platz – home of the Brandenburg Gate – was never going to blend in. However, the designer had a challenge to overcome before construction began for this ambitious project in 1998. Strict building regulations in Berlin state that any construction in this area must not detract from the iconic 18th Century gate. Therefore, Gehry masterminded a design in which the most spectacular feature of the building was concealed from the outside world. The result is a soaring glass atrium in the centre of the edifice, cocooned within a modest limestone façade.

Designed as an exclusive convention centre, Axica hosts everything from gala dinners and conferences to congresses for 600-plus people and those who visit this unique venue rarely fail to be blown away. The Brandenburg Gate – the only surviving historical gate of its kind in Berlin – is one of the city’s most treasured monuments from yesterday, representing peace and unity for the country, and is the first visual feast for everyone who visits this prestigious address. Once inside the DZ Bank, visitors can then witness Gehry’s breathtaking masterpiece – arguably, one of Berlin’s most treasured modern monuments – representing hope, innovation and success for Germany’s future.

As you join us for an evening of celebration, you can look forward to exploring the designer’s theme of motion as you observe architectural features inspired by water and waves, as well as a spectacular conference room, said to resemble a horse’s head. Once you arrive you will understand Axica’s tagline, ‘Expect the unexpected’ and we’re sure you’ll be inspired by this wondrous piece of art which Gehry claimed was ‘The best thing I’ve ever done.’
